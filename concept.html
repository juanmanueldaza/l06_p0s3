AI-generated content is drowning local newsrooms — and they don't have the tools to fight back

A survey of 200 European and North American newsrooms reveals editors spend more time debunking AI slop than producing original journalism. The tools haven't caught up.
Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism · June 2, 2026

A new survey of 200 local newsrooms across Europe and North America reveals a stark reality: 68% of editors report spending more time verifying AI-generated content than producing original reporting. The tools they use — basic reverse image search, manual source tracing — were designed for a pre-generative-AI era.

"We're fighting machines with spreadsheets," said one editor at a mid-size German regional paper with 14 journalists covering a population of 400,000. "The AI slop arrives faster than we can debunk it. Last week we had to retract a story because a fabricated press release fooled two editors before anyone checked the source."

The report identifies a critical gap: while large national newsrooms have adopted AI verification tools, local outlets — which produce the majority of original reporting in most countries — lack the budget, training, and infrastructure to do the same. The result is an asymmetric information war with no front line.

The Asymmetry Problem

The tools used to produce disinformation have advanced by orders of magnitude in the last eighteen months. The tools used to verify information have not. This is not a technology problem — it is an infrastructure problem, and it has a name: the verification gap.
When a single operator with a consumer-grade LLM can generate five hundred convincing press releases in an afternoon, a newsroom staffed by fourteen people with Google search and reverse image lookup has already lost. The ratio is not 2:1 or 10:1. It is closer to 500:0 on any given Tuesday.
What the Reuters Institute survey makes clear is that editors understand this. They feel it every morning when they open their inboxes. What they lack is not awareness — it is leverage.
